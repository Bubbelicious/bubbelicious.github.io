NoWa - No Waste

This school project was a group assignment where we were told to focus on a subject within sustainable living. After some discussion we decided to work with sustainable food consumption with focus on food waste at home.
Team: Maya Otterstål (me), Erik Olsson, Laura Sjölander, Joel Öhrman, Ekaterina Markina

Phase 1 - User research

Quantitative data was gathered with a form that we spread among our different social-groups. Data that was collected were concentrated on both the food waste as well as cooking and shopping habits, to find out where in the process we should focus our effort.

Quality data was gathered with the use of both spontaneous short interviews with customers outside a food market, as well as a few slightly more in-depth interviews with people in our social group.

With the help of an affinity diagram we analysed the data and reached some preliminary conclusions.

Planning your food shopping around your meals should lessen the food waste, and help the ones that are unstructural to be proactive.

When you have less money, you plan your shopping better which makes for less food waste.

Result of research

We learned that when it comes to shopping, people tend to shop for food 2-3 times/week and buy additions when needed, or they shop only when they need something at random times during the week.

Majority of people have a shopping list, but tend to stray from it because of hankering and special prices, and they don’t usually plan ahead their meals and instead cook what they feel like at the time.

What they want to have help with, according to our questions, are structure and planning around what they have at home and when it expires.

The Problem

The average person throw out too much food because of bad planning.
We want to help them with the structure in their day to lessen their waste.

Phase 2 - Concept

We wanted our product to contribute to an easier way in planning the meals and shopping for every household. We wanted it to have an overview of the products that are already in the household and when they expire.

But

We also need it to be easy to use and time effective, at least some degree of auto generating, as well as being able to give cooking inspiration to the user.

Process of product design

Earlier ideas included a device that was to be attached onto the refrigerator, and have a detachable scanner you could use to scan every food product you wanted to keep a closer eye on.

After some discussion and brainstorming among the group, we instead agreed to concentrate on designing an app in which all the functions we wanted to incorporate could be added.

Sketches made by myself and Laura Sjölander

Finished Concept

Bar codes contain product names, best-before dates and expiration warnings (already available at Coop, for example, where store staff receive expiration warnings on products, and can lower the price of products a few days before) so it should not be so difficult to get this information in the app when scanning the code.

The purpose of our app is to reduce food waste in the home with the help of an app that keeps track of items in your refrigerator, pantry when they are about to pass the best-before date, etc.

It assists people with inspiration by searching for recipes based on what is in the consumer's home and it uses food that has already been prepared to in a smart and effective way reduce the risk of saved food having to be thrown away, and help consumers with an overview in their economy.

The app helps private individuals to plan food consumption in the household (function) by providing functions that automate shopping lists based on the menu, and provides an overview of the food budget and all food items in the home, including the food expiration date (mechanism). The user gets a time-efficient tool that reduces personal food waste and thus saves the user money and eases their conscience (value).

What the customer wants to do is buy groceries, cook and eat all the food. (Functional). Furthermore, he wants a good household economy (Emotional) and wants to be viewed as an environmental friend, or at least not one who ignores the environment, and that they want to be perceived as someone who has control (Social).

However, according to our primary type of behavior it is difficult to meet these goals, mainly because they are stressed and unstructured. They often know what would need to be done, but does not get to the structure and planning.

Customer pains

They are usually in the store without a shopping list or plan for what to cook for the week. Purchases go badly, they buy twice as much of something that is already at home or forgets to buy certain products because they do not know what is already at home and what is to be cooked for the week. Food has time to get old and is forgotten at the bottom of the fridge. They throw away food and have a bad conscience about it as it costs money and they know that it is bad for the environment.

Pain reliever: As our product NOWA No Waste, is a mobile app, the customer always have all the functions at hand both at home and in the store. The app syncs the shopping list to what you have at home, it reminds you of the expiration date for what will soon be out and comes automatically with recipe and menu suggestions based on these items. You can also get an overview of your food budget and know what deposits you have made and what you have bought.

Customer Gains

What the customer would like is to save time and money, have a structure to be able to reduce their food waste and ease their conscience. They also need inspiration for everyday cooking, both in the form of recipes and menus.

Gain creator: The functions in the product offer an overview and help with planning. Most things are created automatically (menu, shopping lists, recipes) so they do not get one more thing on the "to-do list" that takes time. Everything is connected to the store card you have at Coop, for example. If the person has used other apps and websites that do not communicate with each other but are used one by one, or maybe lists on paper, then this will now be an app where you have gathered everything. It is an efficient and time-saving tool that brings together all the functions that are about food shopping and cooking in one place.

Phase 3 - Design Prototype

Using Figma, everyone in the group chose one, or in some cases two, functions to focus on. During this process of work, we had several workshops together to make sure that all of our designs were cohesive and worked well with each other. I, personally, were responsible for the functions of Search and Recipes, as well as designing the logo. Once we found the parts satisfactory, it was my responsibility to connect them all into one functioning prototype.

Start

This was the design we settled on for the loggin and home page.
We made sure to use as much familiar shapes and icons as possible, to make it available for every user, even of they have less experience with apps.
The colors we chose (green, grey, orange/yellow), were chosen for the reognizing factor towards eco-friendly and "green" thinking.

Designs made by Erik Olsson and Joel Öhrman respektivly.

Profile

Your profile is linked to an account with Coop/Ica/etc. through the card you choose to use when you shop.
The first thing you enter in the profile is information about your household: How many are you? Are there special diets or allergies to consider? How much time do you want to spend most on cooking your food? This way, the app can sort out the recipes that are not
relevant to you when searching for recipes.
You register your coop / ica card (etc), with bankID of course.
Here you can also choose if you want notifications from the app, and which functions you want notifications from.

Design made by Erik Olsson.

Search for Recipes

The search function work in tandem with the settings in the profile, filtering out recipes that won't be accepted because of dietary or similar needs. Searching out recipes based on an ingredient you have to or want to use, you can chose if you want to use more than one ingredient in the search parameter.
Within the recipe, you can chose if you want to add it to a mealplan or if you want to add it to the list of your favorite recipes.

Designs made by me.

Mealplan

You can make the weekly mealplan yourself based on your saved favorites or you can let the app do it for you based on what you have at home in the fridge, freezer and pantry, the goods that have received an expiration warning and needs to be used soon, as well as your profile settings.

In the listing for the week, you can click on the dish and get the recipe.
While there you can also add / remove today's dish if you want, and when you add recipes to the menu that contain ingredients you do not have at home, you are warned, and you have a function at the bottom of the mealplan that leads directly to creating a shopping list based on what you need for the week's dishes.

Design made by Ekaterina Markina.

Refrigerator

The products in the app are added when they are purchases from the store and are blipped with a barcode function or key. On each item code there is a description of which product it is and the expiration date. That information is entered immediately when the product is scanned and then you can decide which category you want to put it in. The product that are close to expiring are added automatically in the Warning section

Designs made by Erik Olsson.

Budget

You need to log in to the budget function with your mobile BankID for security reasons.
On the front page, you see how much money you have left to buy for this month (from your predetermined budget that you set yourself). If you press the Budget Settings button, you can change the amount per month.

If you press the History button, you can see expenses and deposits. Maybe you have a direct debit, where the food money is deducted from the salary account and deposited in the food account every month. Or you make a manual deposit at the checkout in the grocery store. It is all controlled by your selective grocery card which is linked to the account.

Design made by Laura Sjölander.

Phase 4 - Testing and Iteration

After a handful of user testings we got plenty of data for an iteration. Unfortunately, because of time restrains, we did not have time to do any more iterations then one, but got plenty of positive feedback from the changes we made to the design. From our user tests we got some very valuable observations, as well as the oportunity to see how they interacted with our product.

Start

"It's a bit childish, is it suppose to be a game for children?"

We got some feedback on the design of the login page (color, font, etc.) so we chose to remove the colorful circles in the login image and instead refined the screen with a clean green color and a larger logo.

Design by me

Home

"Is it an app for leftover resturant food? Like there is three slices left from a pizza?"

"Is it a recipe app?"

"Is it about eco food?"

We understood that the affordance was weak. Users misunderstood the purpose of the app.
We therefore removed the recommended recipe to instead focus on the app's unique feature - namely to warn the user of expired items, and we color coded these which tell if they are too old and need to be thrown away or if they can be used in recipes.

Design by me

Profile

"Wow, that's a lot of information to fill in..."

"Do you have to fill this in? I don't wan to..."

"I can't find where I put in my diet"

The profile felt extensive and messy, since it was a single long list. Instead, we grouped the necessary information into two groups: Settings and Notices. Then we also got a place to be able to have a Help function, where you can contact customer service or see a demo of the functions if you skipped it in the first stage. And if you scroll down, you can also find the Favorite recipes.

Design by me

Menu and Icons

"Oh, I thought that one was for the mealplan..." (found shopping list)

"How do you go back to start?"

When we tested the icons, several testers chose the wrong icons to complete the tasks and it caused a lot of confusion.

From left to right: The refrigerator became Home with the app's unique feature of expiration warnings.
Search recipes were moved down as an important function in the middle and Profile moved up in the right corner.
Shopping list got a new icon in the form of grocery bag to better symbolise purchase.

Summary of learning

During this project we have learned a lot about design principles in practice We have learned to follow the users' needs and the importance of testing and iterating, as well as that users discover things that they have not thought of themselves.
One of the more surprising things we learned was that we have produced something new and useful.
According to the majority of our feedback, the app is needed since many have problems with food waste, and that there is an active interest in our product, should we decide to have it developed ("I would download the app if it existed")
